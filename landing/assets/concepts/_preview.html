Dendra — brand mark concepts

Four concepts. Each ties the mark to a different facet of the innovation (name, phase lifecycle, measurement, theorem). All share the Axiom Labs visual vocabulary — 34-px square-capped strokes at 512 viewBox, graphite on warm off-white, a single accent tick in #bf5700. Distinct glyphs, not variations of the origin mark, per brand usage.md portfolio rule.

A stylized upward-branching tree: one trunk, a split node, and two branches. The trunk is the rule; the split is the graduation point; the branches are the two modalities (LLM, ML) that can take over after the evidence clears. One branch tip carries the accent tick — the graduation that's been earned.

Trade-offs

Concept Strength Tension Legibility at 16 px
A · Dendrite Direct name etymology; ties to both "decision tree" and the Greek root. Easiest mark to read as a logo. Could be read as "neural network" branding. Risk of category-cliché if not handled carefully. Excellent — 3 lines + dot + tick, very distinct.
B · Ascend Most literal mapping to the product (six phases IS the product). Unambiguous intellectual claim. Slightly "infographic-y" at small sizes; steps may blur. Reads as a staircase more than as a glyph. Good — the stepped rhythm holds but individual steps become indistinct under ~32 px.
C · Curve Most academically-coherent — matches Figure 1 of the paper. Reads as "measurement" not "marketing." Curves are harder to reproduce consistently across media (print, embroidery, low-DPI screens). More maintenance risk. Fair — the curve reads; the checkpoint dots merge at ~16 px.
D · Gate Most theorem-forward. The mark argues the paper's safety bound visually. Reads well across sizes. Read could drift to "doorway" / "architecture" which is less precise than the other three. Π shape is more generic than a branching tree. Excellent — Π + tick, very distinct even at favicon size.

Recommendation

If you want the mark to say "Dendra" (name-first), pick A · Dendrite. If you want the mark to say what Dendra does (product-first), pick B · Ascend. If you want the mark to anchor to the paper (research-first), pick C · Curve. If you want the mark to argue the safety theorem (claim-first), pick D · Gate.

My personal lean: A · Dendrite for the primary mark (works across every surface, strongest primitive-brand archetype) with C · Curve as a secondary motif for the paper / research contexts. Axiom Labs precedent supports one-mark-per-product; a secondary Curve motif could live as a "research" sub-brand only, not as an alternate logo.
